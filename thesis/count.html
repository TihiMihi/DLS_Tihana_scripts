\section{Introduction}
Experiments are becoming more difficult and people are observing diminishing returns on their experimental data; this is met with efforts in developing more complex equipment. The demands for optimising the usage of these novel apparatuses directly drive the development of complex physical models. Coupled with the advent of powerful computers, the relevance of X-ray ray tracing simulations in designing better experimental equipment is evergrowing. The importance of which will only increase in light of new developments in free electron lasers and 4\textsuperscript{th} generation synchrotrons.


Currently, there exists no robust method for simulating the \acf{pgm}, an essential component of beamlines which require monochromatic light of energies up to ca. 2500 eV. The most effective technique available of deriving the theoretical performance of a beamline is ray-tracing, which in essence is the calculation of the trajectories of photons as they are propagated through an optical system. The same technique is more frequently applied to visible light in the field of computer graphics and 3D animation. Simulation is technically possible presently with ray-tracing softwares like \textit{SHADOW} \cite{SanchezdelRio2011} but such calculations fail to account for the loss of flux due to certain geometrical blockages whilst the \ac{pgm} is in operation, \textit{e.g.} nearing the limits of the energy range of the \ac{pgm}, as well as situations where light beams have been displaced such that they are no longer on the optical surface. Such shortfalls make systematic and iterative calculations of, \textit{i.e.}, transmitted flux as a function of energy extremely difficult. The lack of access to reliable simulation results mean that beamline designers may fail to spot certain problems; this has already led to challenges in operation for beamline B07c at the Diamond Light Source (Diamond), UK's national synchrotron facility. Anecdotal evidence from beamline staff and users suggests a significant problem of higher harmonic contamination. Through the use of the technique developed as part of the work for this thesis, the issue can be confirmed with theoretical calculation. Furthermore, the results can inform an optimised mode of operation. The newly developed methodology which can be applied to simulate any general soft X-ray beamline that has a \ac{pgm} is presented.

In this section, the basic principles of synchrotron radiation are introduced, along with its applicability in the chemical sciences. An introduction to the \ac{pgm} and the ray-tracing technique is discussed to highlight challenges of simulating \acp{pgm}. This sets the stage for understanding specifically the challenge of higher harmonic contamination in soft X-ray beamlines and the detrimental effect it can have on the quality of the data. All of which serve to provide the motivation for developing more robust ray-tracing methodologies that would hopefully resolve potential issues in future beamlines from the outset.

\subsection{Synchrotron Radiation}
Synchrotrons belong to a special class of particle accelerators. In essence, magnetic fields are applied to electrons travelling at relativistic speeds to force a circular trajectory, the energy lost due to the change in acceleration is given off as electromagnetic radiation in the direction of travel, \cref{fig:synchrotron}; it is a special form of \textit{bremsstrahlung} radiation. Electrons are first produced by thermionic emission, which then go through the linear accelerator (Linac). The booster ring further accelerates the electrons to the desired energy, 3 GeV for Diamond, followed by injection to the storage ring. In the storage ring, along the path of the electrons are series of bending magnets and insertion devices. Both of which cause the electrons to change their acceleration and lead to the emission of light. The light is then directed via a series of optics into beamlines, where experiments take place.

\caption{A cartoon depiction of the essential parts of a synchrotron, the arrows in the storage ring indicate the motion of the electrons.}
\label{fig:synchrotron}
\end{figure}

This discovery led to the conception of the first generation synchrotron facilities in the 1970s \cite{Mobilio2015}. Historically, the radiation was used parasitically as the machines were not designed for synchrotron radiation studies. Subsequently, newer synchrotrons were built with the idea that such facilities would be dedicated to light generation. Diamond, where this research was conducted, is an example of a 3\textsuperscript{rd} generation synchrotron which uses undulators and bending magnets for light generation. Despite differences in the property of light produced by these two methods (\S \textbf{spectra}), the archetypal synchrotron light will have the following main properties:
\begin{itemize}
\item high intensity,
\item pulsed with duration on the order of picoseconds,
\item high beam stability,
\item broad spectral range from infrared to hard X-rays.
\end{itemize}
Compared to traditional lab X-ray sources, synchrotrons have superior flux and spectral range. It is these advantages that have led to a huge surge in demand for beam time at synchrotron facilities worldwide. Specifically in the soft X-ray region (photon energies up to ca. 3000 eV), the advent of synchrotrons have enabled capable new techniques that have become ubiquitous in chemistry and material science. There are four overarching themes:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Spectroscopy
\item Diffraction
\item Microscopy
\item Scattering
\end{enumerate}
All of which take advantage of the benefits that synchrotron radiation offers and may be of interest to the modern chemist; spectroscopic techniques are highlighted in specific as to provide relevant background for the subsequent discussion of ray-tracing simulations carried out for a spectroscopy beamline (B07c) at Diamond \cite{Held2020}. Comprehensive reviews are abundant for techniques not discussed here \cite{Wilkins2003,Fink2013,Yano2009,Bergmann2009,Collins2021}.

In the soft X-ray region, spectroscopies can be separated into absorption and emission spectroscopies. \acf{xas} probes primarily the excitation from the ground state to the excited state; \acf{xes} probes the decay from the excited state.  Complementary structural information can usually be obtained by using a combination of techniques. By performing XAS over a range of energies, one can examine both the transition from core electronic states to excited states (LUMO) as well as the continuum where the X-ray delivers sufficient energy for the ejection of an electron. The X-ray absorption spectrum of any material is characterised by a sharp rise in X-ray absorption at specific binding energies. These sharp changes are referred to as absorption edges, which correspond to the minimum energy required to excite a core electron. The absorption by an electron in the $1s$ orbital is referred to as $K$-edge absorption, and $L$-edge for $2s$ or $2p$ electrons. Spectroscopy over the energy range where the edges reside is referred to as \acf{xanes}, which directly probes the density of states at the excited level, consequently is capable of providing extensive information on the oxidation state and coordination of metal atoms of interest \cite{PennerHahn1999}. Over the energy range where a photoelectron is emitted, the technique is \acf{exafs}. An area which is frequently explored using these techniques is to better understand the structure of surfaces. Heterogeneous catalysis, electron correlations are just two of many areas of research made possible by \ac{xanes} and \ac{exafs} \cite{Lee2018,Held2020}. B07c is a part of the versatile soft X-ray beamline at Diamond. Common to almost all work carried out at B07c is the focus on the chemical and compositional properties of surfaces, which are characterised using \ac{xanes} and \ac{exafs}.

\subsection{Monochromation of Soft X-rays}
The requirement for spectral purity in spectroscopic applications is stringent. Specifically in the soft X-ray range, the main method to achieve monochromatic light is by using the dispersive property of a diffraction grating; this process is governed by the grating equation:
\begin{align}
n \lambda &= g (\sin\alpha + \sin\beta) \label{eq:grating}
\end{align}
where $n$ takes integer values and is the diffraction order, $g$ is the grating constant, $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are angles of incidence and diffraction respectively. The parameters are presented in \cref{fig:gratingdiffraction}.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\input{./plots/grating_diffraction.tex}
\centering
\caption{An illustration of a diffraction process from a laminar grating. $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are angles of incidence and diffraction, $g$ is the period of one grating groove.}
\label{fig:gratingdiffraction}
\end{figure}
The periodicity of the grating means that diffracted rays of the same order from adjacent units will be $g\sin\alpha$ out of phase. Constructive interference requires that the path difference be an integer multiple of the wavelength that is of interest. In operation, the user does not usually specify the angles $\theta$, $\alpha$, and $\beta$ independently. Through the introduction of the quantity fixed-focus constant ($\cff$):
\begin{align}
\cff = \frac{\cos\beta}{\cos\alpha} \label{eq:cff}
\end{align}
which the user specifies, along with the desired energy, the angles can be trivially computed. This is known as the fixed $\cff$ mode of operation and is the normal mode of operation. An updated grating equation in terms of only the angle $\beta$ and $\cff$ can be obtained by algebraic manipulation. Applying the Pythagorean identity to \cref{eq:cff}, one obtains:
\begin{align*}
\sin^2\alpha &= 1 - \cos^2 \alpha \\
&= 1- \left(\frac{\cos^2\alpha}{\cos^2\beta}\right) \cos^2\beta \\
&= 1- \frac{\cos^2\beta}{\cff^2} \numberthis. \label{eq:sinalpha}
\end{align*}
By squaring both sides of \cref{eq:grating} and substituting \cref{eq:sinalpha}, one can obtain the updated grating equation:
\begin{align*}
\left(\frac{n\lambda}{g} - \sin\beta\right)^2 &= \sin^2\alpha \\
\left(\frac{n\lambda}{g} - \sin\beta\right)^2 &= 1- \frac{\cos^2\beta}{\cff^2} \\
\Aboxed{1 - \left(\frac{n\lambda}{g} - \sin\beta\right)^2 &= \frac{\cos^2\beta}{\cff^2}} \numberthis. \label{eq:cffgrating}
\end{align*}
\cref{eq:cffgrating} is in essence a quadratic equation in terms of $\sin\beta$, which can be solved given that all the other parameters are known. $\alpha$ can be computed from $\beta$ and the specified $\cff$. The angle $\theta$ is obtained by imposing the constraint that the incoming and outgoing beams must remain parallel. This gives $2\theta = \alpha - \beta$. 

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\input{./plots/pgmoperation.tex}
\caption{A popular SX-700 style \ac{pgm} designed by Petersen \textit{et al.} \cite{Petersen1995}. Demonstrating the rotations of mirror and grating to obtain different energies ($E$ and $E'$) in the fixed $\cff$ mode. In the above scenario, $\sin\alpha/\sin\beta = \sin\alpha'/\sin\beta' \equiv \cff$ remains constant, however, $E' > E$. Without loss of generality, $\alpha\neq\alpha'$, $\theta\neq\theta'$, and $\beta\neq\beta'$.}
\label{fig:pgmoperation}
\end{figure}

The mechanical evolution of such a setup may be unintuitive, \cref{fig:pgmoperation} shows two configurations of the same \ac{pgm} operating at a fixed $\cff$ to give two different energies. By maintaining the same $\cff$, a scan over energy is performed by rotating the mirror and grating in a conspired way as to effectively change $\beta$. Limitations are reached when the beam is no longer incident on the mirror or the grating or the beam footprint becomes too large and flux begins to deteriorate.

\subsection{Challenges in \ac{pgm} Operation and Ray-Tracing}
Here, an account of challenges both in \ac{pgm} operation as well as ray-tracing for \acp{pgm} are presented. This provides the justification for the need of better methodologies.


During the design stage of a \ac{pgm}, certain geometrical offsets are to be fixed to allow the installation of mechanical components and can't be altered later. These offsets dictate the essential properties such as the energy range as well as its efficiency over said range. Ideally, a set of optimised parameters is chosen. A typical schematic is presented in \cref{fig:pgm}.
\begin{figure}
\input{./plots/pgm.tex}
\caption{A schematic of a typical modern \ac{pgm} geometry. Quantities highlighted with red arrows are to be fixed prior to manufacturing. Figure adapted from unpublished work by Dr T.-L Lee \cite{Lee}.}
\label{fig:pgm}
\end{figure}

Despite the best efforts, flux can be inadvertently lost in a number of places during normal operation:
\begin{enumerate}
\item efficiencies of optical elements,
\item size of the beam footprint on the optical elements being larger than the elements themselves,
\item and geometrical blockages.
\end{enumerate}

At Diamond and many other synchrotron facilities, the software package \textit{SHADOW} \cite{SanchezdelRio2011} is frequently used to carry out ray-tracing calculations. Currently, SHADOW{} is capable of accounting for both reflectivities of the mirror coating material (common ones include Rh, Si, Pt, \textit{etc.}) as well as the slope error, which are imperfections of the optical surface, through its preprocessors \textit{PREREFL} and \textit{WAVINESS}. However, SHADOW{} is incapable of handling geometrical blockages. The two possible scenarios where this might happen is presented in \cref{fig:blockage}. Due to the design principle of the underlying SHADOW{} codebase, ray-tracing is done in the sequence specified by the user. In normal \ac{pgm} operation, the correct sequence met by the rays is mirror followed by grating. In both scenarios in \cref{fig:blockage}, the sequence is broken. Currently, SHADOW{} has no \textit{a priori} knowledge of the global optical setup and therefore is restricted by the assumption of sequentiality. This thesis proposes a solution which overcomes this fundamental limitation of the software, which will be discussed in \textbf{\S Methodology}.

At present, both grating efficiencies and flux calculations have to handled with external packages \cite{Johnson2022,Walters,Tanaka2001}. This presents a significant workload where manual adjustments of dozens of parameters are needed. Grating efficiency will vary as a function of both $\cff$ and diffraction order.
